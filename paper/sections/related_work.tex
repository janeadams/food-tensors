\section{Related Work}

This work builds on the Cube Rule as a prior food-classification framework and introduces two external strands of literature to formalize the proposed tensor-based approach.
The first strand is modern LLM control and prompting: prompt taxonomies, schema-constrained generation, grammar-constrained decoding, guided decoding, and retrieval-augmented generation provide language for the prompt design, structured decoding, exemplar use, and post-generation filtering described in the Methods section.
In this paper those ideas appear concretely when the model is asked for a dish matching a specific cell such as $(\text{toast}, \text{seafood}, \text{rice})$ and is steered toward outputs like nigiri rather than loosely related rice dishes or free-form descriptions.
The second strand is tensor and geometry-oriented representation theory: tensor completion, manifold hypotheses, simplicial complex constructions, persistent homology, and topological data analysis provide language for discussing empty-cell completion, clustered occupancy structure, and topological features of the support set $\mathcal{S} = \{(i,j,k) : T_{i,j,k}=1\}$ in higher-order categorical space.
Here the motivating examples are food-level rather than abstract: filling an empty cell with a plausible named dish is treated as tensor completion, while repeated occupancies around nearby dishes such as nigiri, sushi rolls, and ramen motivate the geometric language for local structure.
Classical combinatorics references are also relevant for occupancy guarantees, including direct pigeonhole-style collision arguments when the number of foods exceeds $|I \times J \times K| = IJK$.
Taken together, these literatures provide the methodological and conceptual vocabulary used to define, populate, and analyze the proposed taxonomy.
