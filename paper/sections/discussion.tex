\begin{theorem}[No Free Lunch Special]
If the number of distinct foods assigned to tensor cells exceeds $IJK$, then at least one coordinate $(i,j,k)$ must contain more than one food.
Equivalently, if $|\RealFoods| > IJK$ (or if any subset of $\ConceivableFoods$ of size $> IJK$ is mapped into the index space), then collisions are unavoidable.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Proof (sketch)]
There are exactly $IJK$ available cells in the tensor.
Assigning more than $IJK$ distinct foods to those cells forces at least one cell to receive two or more foods by the pigeonhole principle~\cite{pigeonhole}.
\end{proof}
\textit{Example.} A concrete collision can occur at $(i,j,k) = (\text{nachos}, \text{red\_meat}, \text{pasta\_noodle})$, where both pasta salad with ground beef and spaghetti with meatballs plausibly occupy the same cell.
Such a case suggests that one might introduce a fourth dimension $\ell$ for temperature or a fifth dimension $m$ for protein morphology if finer distinctions are desired.

\subsection{Collisions and Ontology Size}

This is mathematically trivial but operationally useful: once the catalog grows past the available cell count, collisions cease to be a modeling accident and become a structural consequence of the ontology.
At that point the ontology begins to boil over: the problem is no longer too many cooks in the kitchen, but too many dishes on the same plate.
This motivates optional extensions with additional axes (e.g., a fourth index $\ell$), which can be interpreted as moving from $T \in \{0,1\}^{I \times J \times K}$ toward a higher-order multiway representation.
For example, future work could introduce additional dimensions such as vegetable type (categorical) or spice level (ordinal or quantitative, depending on how pungency is formalized), but doing so would require explicit decisions about data type and measurement scale before such axes could be used to disambiguate colliding items.
One especially promising extension is a cuisine-type or cultural-origin axis. Motivated in part by the informal cross-cultural observation we will call the ``Every Country Has a Dumpling Theory''~\cite{ask_europe_dumpling}, such an axis could help identify morphologically similar $(i,j,k)$ combinations that are common in some culinary traditions but absent in others. It could also surface cross-cultural analogies and speculative transfers more carefully, provided that the model does not treat religious dietary constraints or other culturally meaningful prohibitions as ordinary missing data to be imputed away.
One practical tension is model granularity: under-specified ontologies (small $I$, $J$, $K$) collapse semantically distinct foods into the same $(i,j,k)$, while over-specified ontologies create extreme sparsity ($|\mathcal{S}|/IJK$ very small) and weak empirical support per coordinate.
The challenge is to add enough garnish to separate meaningful distinctions without overseasoning the ontology into extreme sparsity.
That same trade-off appears inside existing axes: the current \texttt{other\_grain} starch bucket is convenient, but expanding it to distinguish grains such as teff and quinoa could reduce ambiguity and reveal new neighborhoods of the tensor. Such a refinement might separate established regional foods more cleanly while also making room for plausible cross-category completions, for example an injera burrito built around a teff-based wrapper rather than treating all non-wheat, non-rice grains as the same starch substrate.

\subsection{Scope of the Ontology}
\label{sec:candy-salad-discussion}

Future extensions should resolve boundary cases where sugar-dominant processed inclusions (for example nougat/candy in snickers salad) may trigger non-salad intuitions without mapping cleanly to the current starch index $k$.
This paper restricts attention to edible foods, even though the original Cube Rule also discusses non-food cases and famously proposes, for example, that humans are ravioli (that is, calzones under the Rule's morphology).
An expanded ontology with additional starch and protein categories might therefore reveal interesting findings outside ordinary cuisine as well: a leatherbound book, for instance, could be modeled as an inverse taco in which a protein-like exterior surrounds a filling of starch.
We also restrict the morphological axis to ordinary three-dimensional food geometry and do not attempt higher-dimensional morphology.
Future work could ask whether tesseract-like foods or other higher-order culinary solids require an additional extension of the cube axis itself, effectively promoting morphology to a richer higher-dimensional index.

\subsection{Boundary Cases}

The horseshoe theory of salad is an entertaining consequence of the current Cube Rule, but it also exposes a crack in the epistemological crust of the ontology. Mashed potatoes are treated as salad because they are a starch-based mono-food; by the same reasoning, a bowl of croutons appears to revert from nachos back to salad once the lettuce disappears. Yet the toast sandwich is canonically classified as sandwich rather than salad even though it, too, is effectively a mono-starch food, because its starch occupies top and bottom faces rather than none. Plain rice remains even less settled: it is the one food for which the Rule explicitly declines to decide.

Taken together, these cases suggest that morphology alone does not cleanly resolve mono-starch foods. Under the current ontology, one mono-starch becomes salad, another remains sandwich, and a third is left unclassified. This is no longer just a funny edge case but a genuine modeling problem: the present nine categories do not distinguish between dishes whose ambiguity comes from mixed ingredients and dishes whose ambiguity comes from being composed of starch alone. In that sense the analogy to political horseshoe theory cuts twice: just as critics argue that horseshoe theory is reductive because it compresses emergent factions into an overly simple geometry, the Cube Rule also compresses mono-starches into categories built for more familiar morphologies rather than making room for them on their own terms~\cite{horseshoe}. The issue is not merely quirky; it remains slightly underdone at the center.

That tension motivates a tenth cube category reserved for \emph{mono-starches} or \emph{single-starch} foods. Such a category would be distinct from both salad (no structural starch) and nachos (outer container plus inner starch pieces), and would absorb plain rice, plain croutons, mashed potatoes, and related starch-based mono-foods without forcing them into categories whose logic was designed for other structures. Under that extension, the horseshoe theory would remain useful as a description of how the current Cube Rule behaves, while the new category would serve as a cleaner repair to the ontology itself.

Our current dish-level classification does not add that tenth category, so we preserve the Rule's open interpretation for mono-starch boundary cases in the reported tensor.
Even that repair would leave at least one potato-shaped problem on the table: where should potato-filled pierogis go? Structurally they are calzone-like, since starch encloses the filling from the outside; by filling logic they also look nacho-like, since the interior is itself starch; and under a mono-starch extension they might be claimed as starch through and through. We leave that exercise to the reader.
Relatedly, jello-style dishes suggest that gelatin may warrant a separate protein index $j$ beyond the current formalism, likely yielding a very sparse but interesting subset of $\mathcal{S}$ in the morphological state space (with soup dumplings as one motivating example, since they classify as calzone under Cube Rule morphology and include gelatinized broth).

\subsection{Topological Interpretation}

Topological language is useful here: regions where $T_{i,j,k}=1$ is dense in index space behave like low-curvature neighborhoods, while isolated $(i,j,k) \in \mathcal{S}$ resemble high-curvature outliers in a discrete morphological state space; for example, several bread-based categories may cluster while a more unusual dish such as jello salad sits farther from those neighborhoods.
If local adjacency relations are imposed on occupied cells, $\mathcal{S}$ can also be viewed as inducing a sparse simplicial complex over the index lattice, with missing bridges and cavities corresponding to structurally absent combinations.
Under that view, persistent homology and broader topological data analysis provide language for discussing connected components, holes, and other mesoscale features of the food-occupancy pattern as the ontology grows, such as whether sushi-like foods form one connected region while dessert salads occupy scattered outlier cells.
Operationally, the ontology should avoid both excessive coarseness and excessive fragmentation: $I$, $J$, and $K$ should be large enough to separate distinct dishes, but restrained enough to keep $|\mathcal{S}|$ empirically supportable.

\section{Speculative Food Fiction}

One practical use of the framework is imputation: once a neighborhood of the tensor is well populated, nearby empty cells become prompts for novel foods that are morphologically coherent even if they are not yet culturally established.
The most productive region for this kind of food fiction is arguably the calzone morphology.
If one asks, objectively, which morphology is most beloved, calzone has a strong claim: it contains dumplings, ravioli, empanadas, and other durable classics, while also absorbing newer handheld starch technologies such as Pop-Tarts and Uncrustables~\cite{arko_cube_rule_food}.
That pattern is not accidental.
Fully enclosed starches travel well, protect fillings, and are easy to eat without utensils, so the calzone region of the tensor is both historically crowded and structurally hospitable to innovation.

This makes calzone a natural target for speculative completion.
Given a desired protein-starch pairing, the framework can ask the LLM to impute a missing but morphologically valid enclosed dish, then accept only outputs whose descriptions preserve the six-face enclosure required by the Cube Rule.

These examples are intentionally modest rather than fantastical.
That modesty exposes an epistemic limitation in the setup: because $\RealFoods$ is a theoretical historical set that we only approximate with a finite catalog, an output proposed for a cell in $\mathcal{E}$ need not truly lie in $\ConceivableFoods \setminus \RealFoods$.
It may instead be a real but rare, poorly documented, regionally specific, or simply uncatalogued food that was missing from the menu of our current dataset.
In other words, the pipeline is better understood as generating candidates at the frontier between the known support of the catalog and the larger, only partially observed set $\RealFoods$.

This raises a natural limiting question: as culinary innovation accumulates over long time scales, does $\RealFoods$ asymptotically approach $\ConceivableFoods$, making genuinely empty cells increasingly scarce?
We do not answer that here.
But the framework remains useful under either view.
If the gap $\ConceivableFoods \setminus \RealFoods$ stays large, then the method is a tool for genuine novelty imputation; if the gap is small, then it is still a productive way to surface interesting, low-attestation, or culturally peripheral combinations that inhabit underexplored regions of the tensor.
In that weaker but still valuable sense, speculative food fiction is less about inventing nonsense than about finding foods that the morphology suggests ought to exist, whether or not history has already gotten there first. The point is less to spice things up for its own sake than to probe which empty cells are structurally plausible.
