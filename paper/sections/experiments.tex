\section{Experiments}

The experiment workflow has three steps: map observed foods to coordinates $(i,j,k)$ and set $T_{i,j,k}=1$ accordingly; measure occupancy $|\mathcal{S}|$ and collisions (multiple foods at the same $(i,j,k)$); and generate candidate foods for cells in $\mathcal{E}$.
The third step can be interpreted as a constrained categorical form of tensor completion: we do not impute arbitrary values, but instead propose named dishes for previously unoccupied cells under hard ontological constraints, such as asking for a plausible dish at a previously empty cube/protein/starch combination rather than assigning an anonymous latent value.
Initial analysis reports which regions of the index set $\{1,\ldots,I\} \times \{1,\ldots,J\} \times \{1,\ldots,K\}$ are dense ($T_{i,j,k}=1$ for many $(i,j,k)$ in a neighborhood), sparse, or structurally inconsistent under the chosen ontology; the sparse regions can also be read as zones of uneaten potential.
From a representation-learning perspective, this is a small-scale study of combinatorial generalization under hard axis constraints on $i$, $j$, and $k$: the model must recombine familiar ingredients and morphologies into specific candidates such as a new toast-type seafood/rice dish or a new quiche-type dairy/pastry dish.
Empirically, the workflow tests whether culinary naming conventions can be mapped consistently onto the proposed coordinate system.
Axis categories in Figure~\ref{fig:tensor-projections-speculated} and Figure~\ref{fig:tensor-3d-speculated} are ordered by occupancy-profile hierarchical clustering as described in the Methods section (applied to the indices $i$, $j$, $k$ along each axis).

\subsection{The Candy Salad Conundrum}

We also run a targeted analysis of foods whose names explicitly contain ``salad'' (for example, chicken salad, egg salad, Snickers salad\footnote{Snickers salad is a dessert-style potluck dish, commonly made with chopped Snickers bars, tart apples, and whipped topping (often with pudding), and is widely associated with Upper Midwest potluck and church-supper traditions (especially Minnesota and Iowa), though no single point of origin is well documented.}, and jello salad\footnote{Jello salad is a molded gelatin dish, often containing fruit, marshmallows, or whipped topping, that appears in church-supper, holiday, and potluck traditions across the Upper Midwest and other parts of the United States; despite the name, its structure is far removed from the vegetable-forward sense of ``salad.''}), asking the model to classify each item by structural starch placement and thus to assign a cube index $i$ (and implicitly $j$, $k$).
Table~\ref{tab:salad-cube-rule} reports only the subset that violates the Cube Rule salad definition (no structural starch faces), which in the tensor corresponds to the `salad` morphology with \texttt{starch\_type=none}.
We test the hypothesis that many such violations arise from discrete starch inclusions (for example, croutons, pasta pieces, or candy chunks), which shift morphology from the salad index $i_{\text{salad}}$ toward the Cube Rule ``nachos'' index $i_{\text{nachos}}$ (outer dish plus inner starch).
